## 1. What Is It? (Theory)

### The Core Idea: Meaning as Coordinates

Imagine a map where every concept occupies a position in space. Concepts that are semantically
related are positioned near each other; unrelated concepts are far apart.

An **embedding** is exactly this: a position in a high-dimensional space that encodes the meaning
of a piece of text (or image, audio, or code). The position is represented as a vector of numbers
— typically 384 to 3,072 floating-point values.

```
"dog"        → [0.12, -0.45, 0.78, 0.03, ...]   (1536 dimensions)
"puppy"      → [0.13, -0.44, 0.77, 0.04, ...]   (close to "dog")
"cat"        → [0.11, -0.42, 0.71, 0.09, ...]   (close to "dog" — both pets)
"automobile" → [0.53,  0.21, -0.34, 0.67, ...]  (far from "dog")
"car"        → [0.54,  0.22, -0.33, 0.68, ...]  (close to "automobile")
```

This is why semantic search works: "What cars have the best fuel economy?" and "Which automobiles
are most fuel-efficient?" produce vectors that are close to the same nearby documents — even though
they share no keywords.

### The Problem This Solves

**Keyword search** (traditional search) operates on exact matches:
```
Query: "canine behavior problems"
Misses: documents about "dog aggression" or "puppy training issues"
        → Shares no keywords with the query
```

**Semantic search** (embedding-based) operates on meaning:
```
Query: "canine behavior problems"  →  embed  →  vector
Documents about "dog aggression"   →  embed  →  nearby vector  → returned!
Documents about "puppy training"   →  embed  →  nearby vector  → returned!
```

The embedding captures what the text *means*, not which words it *contains*.

## 2. How It Actually Works — Deep Mechanics

### 2.1 From Tokens to a Fixed-Size Vector

An embedding model is a transformer (or similar architecture) trained to produce a single
fixed-size vector from a variable-length text input.

**How it collapses a sequence to one vector:**

```
Text: "The quick brown fox"
    │
    ▼ Tokenize
["The", "quick", "brown", "fox"]  (4 tokens)
    │
    ▼ Token embeddings (each token → 768-dim vector)
4 vectors, 768 dimensions each
    │
    ▼ Transformer encoder (all tokens attend to each other)
4 contextual vectors (each token now aware of context)
    │
    ▼ Pooling (collapse 4 vectors → 1 vector)
Options:
  CLS pooling:  use the [CLS] token vector (BERT-style)
  Mean pooling: average all token vectors (often better in practice)
  Max pooling:  take max per dimension across tokens
    │
    ▼ Optional: normalize to unit length
Final embedding: 768-dim vector
```

**Why pooling matters:** Different pooling strategies produce different embedding qualities.
Mean pooling over all tokens typically outperforms CLS pooling for retrieval tasks. Most modern
embedding models use mean pooling with optional L2 normalization.

### 2.2 Contextual Embeddings vs Word Embeddings

**Word2Vec / GloVe (2013–2015):** One vector per word, regardless of context.
```
"bank" (financial) → same vector as "bank" (river bank)
```
These are **static embeddings**: each word has one fixed representation.

**BERT / modern embedding models (2018+):** Context-aware vectors.
```
"I went to the bank to deposit money"  →  "bank" here gets a finance-oriented vector
"The river bank was muddy"             →  "bank" here gets a geography-oriented vector
```
These are **contextual embeddings**: the representation of a word depends on the entire sentence.

For retrieval tasks, contextual sentence-level embeddings dramatically outperform word-level
embeddings. When you call an embedding API today, you're getting contextual embeddings.

### 2.3 What Training Teaches the Embedding Space

Embedding models are trained on a **contrastive objective**: similar texts should have similar
vectors (pulled together), dissimilar texts should have dissimilar vectors (pushed apart).

The most common training approach in 2024–2026:

```
Contrastive learning with hard negatives:

For each (anchor, positive) pair:
  - anchor:   "What is photosynthesis?"
  - positive: "Photosynthesis is the process by which plants..."  ← genuinely relevant
  - negative: "Photosynthesis" (article title, too easy to distinguish)
  - hard_neg: "Chemosynthesis is a process by which organisms..." ← harder to distinguish

Training loss pushes:
  cos(anchor, positive) → high (~0.9)
  cos(anchor, negative) → low  (~0.1)
  cos(anchor, hard_neg) → low  (but harder to push — these are the ones that matter)
```

Hard negatives (texts that are superficially similar but actually irrelevant) are what make
embedding models good at retrieval. Models trained with many hard negatives per pair have
much better retrieval quality on difficult queries.

### 2.4 The Geometry of Meaning

The embedding space has exploitable structure. Classic examples from Word2Vec:

```
vec("king") - vec("man") + vec("woman") ≈ vec("queen")
vec("Paris") - vec("France") + vec("Germany") ≈ vec("Berlin")
```

More practical for retrieval:
```
vec("iPhone") ≈ vec("Apple smartphone")
vec("ML") ≈ vec("machine learning") ≈ vec("neural networks")
vec("heart attack") ≈ vec("myocardial infarction")  ← important for medical search!
```

The model has learned that these phrases occupy the same region of the 1,536-dimensional space.

### 2.5 Dimensionality: The Numbers That Matter

| Dimensions | Storage per embedding | Good for |
|---|---|---|
| 384 (small) | 1.5 KB | On-device, tight memory budget |
| 768 (medium) | 3 KB | General purpose; good quality |
| 1,536 (OpenAI small) | 6 KB | Strong quality; standard OpenAI |
| 3,072 (OpenAI large) | 12 KB | Best quality; expensive |

At 1 million documents:
- 768 dimensions: 3 GB just for embedding storage
- 3,072 dimensions: 12 GB just for embedding storage

Higher dimensions = better quality but more storage and slower search. Matryoshka embeddings
(OpenAI text-embedding-3 models) let you truncate to fewer dimensions with graceful quality
degradation — giving you the flexibility to trade quality for speed at query time.

## 3. Real-World Context

### Where Embeddings Power Production Systems

| System | What's Embedded | How Used |
|---|---|---|
| Spotify Discover Weekly | Songs, listening history | Recommend similar songs |
| Pinterest visual search | Images | Find visually similar pins |
| GitHub Copilot | Code context | Retrieve relevant code examples |
| Google Search (neural) | Queries, documents | Semantic relevance scoring |
| Airbnb listings | Listing descriptions + guest history | Recommend relevant listings |
| ChatGPT memory | Conversation history | Retrieve relevant past conversations |
| RAG systems everywhere | Document chunks | Find relevant context for LLM |
| E-commerce search | Product descriptions | Match queries to products |

### The Retrieval Problem at Scale

Finding the nearest embedding to a query vector across 1 million documents requires computing
1 million cosine similarities. At 1,536 dimensions, that's 1.5 billion multiplications per query.
At 100 queries/second, that's 150 billion multiplications per second — clearly impractical for
exact search.

This is why **Approximate Nearest Neighbor (ANN)** algorithms exist, and why **vector databases**
are not just regular databases with an embedding column. More on this in Sections 6.3 and 6.4.

## 4. Code Examples

### Getting Your First Embedding

```python
# pip install anthropic openai sentence-transformers

# === OpenAI Embeddings ===
from openai import OpenAI

client = OpenAI()

def embed_openai(text: str, model: str = "text-embedding-3-small") -> list[float]:
    response = client.embeddings.create(
        model=model,
        input=text,
        encoding_format="float"
    )
    return response.data[0].embedding

vec = embed_openai("What is photosynthesis?")
print(f"Dimensions: {len(vec)}")      # 1536 for text-embedding-3-small
print(f"First 5 values: {vec[:5]}")
print(f"Storage: {len(vec) * 4} bytes per embedding")  # float32 = 4 bytes

# === Voyage AI (Anthropic-recommended) ===
# pip install voyageai
import voyageai

vo = voyageai.Client()  # uses VOYAGE_API_KEY env var

def embed_voyage(texts: list[str], input_type: str = "query") -> list[list[float]]:
    """
    input_type: "query" for questions/queries, "document" for content being indexed.
    Voyage (and Cohere) use asymmetric embeddings — different processing for each.
    """
    result = vo.embed(texts, model="voyage-3-large", input_type=input_type)
    return result.embeddings

# Query embedding
q_vec = embed_voyage(["What is photosynthesis?"], input_type="query")[0]

# Document embeddings (for indexing)
doc_vecs = embed_voyage([
    "Photosynthesis is the process plants use to convert light to food.",
    "The stock market closed higher on Tuesday."
], input_type="document")

print(f"Query vector dims:    {len(q_vec)}")
print(f"Document vector dims: {len(doc_vecs[0])}")

# === Local / Free: sentence-transformers ===
from sentence_transformers import SentenceTransformer

model = SentenceTransformer("all-MiniLM-L6-v2")   # 80MB model, free, 384 dims

texts = ["This is a test sentence.", "Another example sentence."]
embeddings = model.encode(texts, normalize_embeddings=True)
print(f"Shape: {embeddings.shape}")    # (2, 384)
```

### Understanding the Embedding Space

```python
import numpy as np
from openai import OpenAI

client = OpenAI()

def embed(text: str) -> np.ndarray:
    resp = client.embeddings.create(model="text-embedding-3-small", input=text)
    return np.array(resp.data[0].embedding)

def cosine_similarity(a: np.ndarray, b: np.ndarray) -> float:
    return float(np.dot(a, b) / (np.linalg.norm(a) * np.linalg.norm(b)))

# Demonstrate semantic similarity
pairs = [
    ("dog", "puppy"),                          # very similar
    ("dog", "cat"),                            # related (both pets)
    ("dog", "automobile"),                     # unrelated
    ("car", "automobile"),                     # synonyms
    ("heart attack", "myocardial infarction"), # medical synonyms
    ("What is Python?", "Python programming language overview"), # query vs doc
]

print("Semantic similarity scores:")
for a, b in pairs:
    vec_a = embed(a)
    vec_b = embed(b)
    sim = cosine_similarity(vec_a, vec_b)
    print(f"  '{a}' ↔ '{b}': {sim:.3f}")

# Expected output:
# "dog" ↔ "puppy":              ~0.85  (high)
# "dog" ↔ "cat":                ~0.70  (moderate)
# "dog" ↔ "automobile":         ~0.35  (low)
# "car" ↔ "automobile":         ~0.92  (very high — synonyms)
# "heart attack" ↔ "myocardial infarction": ~0.78  (medical knowledge!)
```

### Batch Embedding (Production Pattern)

```python
from openai import OpenAI
import numpy as np
from typing import Iterator

client = OpenAI()

def embed_batch(
    texts: list[str],
    model: str = "text-embedding-3-small",
    batch_size: int = 100,    # OpenAI max is 2048 per call, but smaller batches are safer
) -> np.ndarray:
    """
    Embed a large list of texts in batches.
    Returns numpy array of shape (len(texts), n_dims).
    """
    all_embeddings = []
    
    for i in range(0, len(texts), batch_size):
        batch = texts[i : i + batch_size]
        
        response = client.embeddings.create(model=model, input=batch)
        
        # Responses may not be in order — sort by index
        sorted_data = sorted(response.data, key=lambda x: x.index)
        batch_embeddings = [d.embedding for d in sorted_data]
        all_embeddings.extend(batch_embeddings)
        
        print(f"Embedded {min(i + batch_size, len(texts))}/{len(texts)} texts...")
    
    return np.array(all_embeddings)


# Embed 1000 documents
documents = [f"Document about topic {i}" for i in range(1000)]
embeddings = embed_batch(documents, batch_size=100)
print(f"Embedding matrix shape: {embeddings.shape}")  # (1000, 1536)
print(f"Total storage: {embeddings.nbytes / 1024:.0f} KB")
```

## 6. Alternatives & Comparisons

### Sparse vs Dense Embeddings

| Type | Example | How It Works | Strengths | Weaknesses |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Sparse** (BM25, TF-IDF) | `{word: count}` dict | Exact word matching with frequency weighting | Exact match, fast, interpretable | Misses synonyms, context |
| **Dense** (BERT, text-embedding-3) | `[0.1, -0.3, ...]` float array | Neural network: meaning → coordinates | Semantic matching | Misses exact terms, slower |
| **Sparse neural** (SPLADE) | Sparse learned vocabulary | Neural network maps to sparse space | Both semantic + keyword | Complex to run |

Dense embeddings are the default for semantic search. Sparse (BM25) is not dead — it's the
complement to dense in hybrid search (Section 6.6).

### Static vs Contextual Embeddings

| Type | Example | Quality | Speed |
|---|---|---|---|
| Static word embeddings | Word2Vec, GloVe | Poor | Very fast |
| Contextual sentence embeddings | BERT, sentence-transformers | Good | Moderate |
| LLM-based embeddings | text-embedding-3, voyage-3 | Best | Slower |

For retrieval: always use contextual sentence embeddings or better. Static embeddings produce
significantly worse retrieval results and are not recommended for new systems.

## 7. Trade-offs

### More Dimensions vs Storage + Speed

**Higher dimensions (3,072):**
- Marginally better retrieval quality on hard queries
- 2× more storage, 2× slower similarity computation

**Matryoshka / truncated (512 dims from a 3,072 model):**
- ~5–10% quality loss at 512 dims vs full 3,072
- 6× less storage, 6× faster search
- OpenAI text-embedding-3 supports this natively

**Rule:** Start with `text-embedding-3-small` (1,536 dims). Only upgrade to large if evals show
meaningful retrieval improvement on your specific dataset.

### API vs Local Model

**API:**
- Zero infrastructure, immediate start
- $0.02–$0.13 per million tokens depending on model
- At 100M tokens/month = $2,000–$13,000/month

**Local (sentence-transformers):**
- Free per token (just compute)
- ~0.5–2ms per embedding on CPU (MiniLM)
- Requires model hosting, dependency management

**Rule:** Start with API. Move to local when costs exceed $500/month OR when data privacy
requires it.

## 8. When To Use vs. When To Avoid

### Embeddings for Search
✅ Use when: Users search by meaning, not exact keywords (natural language queries)
✅ Use when: You need to match queries across synonyms, languages, or paraphrases
✅ Use when: Building RAG (embedding is mandatory for the retrieval step)
❌ Avoid when: Users search by exact identifiers (product codes, order numbers) → use BM25/keyword
❌ Avoid when: You need zero-latency lookups on known keys → use a cache/database

### Embedding Model Choice
✅ Voyage for: Domain-specific text (medical, legal, financial, code)
✅ OpenAI for: General English text, fast iteration, Matryoshka flexibility
✅ Cohere for: Cost-sensitive high-volume; best price/quality ratio
✅ Local for: Privacy requirements, high-volume where API costs are prohibitive

⚠️ Common mistakes:
- Using the same model for queries and documents without checking if the model is asymmetric
  (Voyage and Cohere distinguish query vs document; OpenAI doesn't — use the right input_type)
- Embedding very short text (1–2 words) — short texts have noisy, unreliable embeddings
- Not normalizing embeddings before cosine similarity — unnormalized vectors require more computation
- Assuming text-embedding-3-large is always better — for many tasks, small is within 2–3% and 6× cheaper

## 9. Senior AI Engineer Perspective

### The "Garbage In, Garbage Out" Problem

The embedding of a sentence encodes its meaning. If your document chunks are garbage — too long,
too short, cut mid-sentence, mixing unrelated topics — the embeddings will be meaningless averages.

The most common retrieval quality problem I've seen isn't the embedding model. It's the chunks.
A 2,000-token chunk that covers five different topics produces an embedding that's the average
of all five topics — it won't be close to any specific query about any one of them.

Fix your chunks first (Section 6.5). Then evaluate model quality.

### Embeddings Are Your Schema

In a relational database, schema design determines query performance. In a retrieval system,
the embedding model (and chunking strategy) IS your schema. Changing the embedding model requires
re-embedding the entire corpus. This is expensive at scale.

**Choose your embedding model deliberately**, not by grabbing the first one you find. Run a small
offline evaluation: embed 50 representative queries and 500 representative documents, compute
retrieval metrics (Recall@5, nDCG@10). That 2-hour investment will save weeks of production
debugging later.

### The Interview Question That Shows Deep Understanding

"Explain how cosine similarity works and why we use it instead of Euclidean distance for embeddings."

Answer:
> Cosine similarity measures the angle between two vectors, not their length. Embeddings from
> transformer models can vary in magnitude based on text length, specificity, and other factors
> unrelated to semantic meaning. Two sentences with the same meaning but different lengths will
> have similar *directions* but different *magnitudes*. Cosine similarity captures the directional
> alignment (meaning similarity) while being invariant to magnitude differences. Euclidean distance
> would penalize vectors of different lengths even if they point in the same direction. In practice,
> normalizing vectors to unit length first and then using dot product is equivalent to cosine
> similarity and is faster to compute.
