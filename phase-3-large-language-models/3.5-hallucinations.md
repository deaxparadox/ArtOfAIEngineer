## 1. What Is It? (Theory)

### The Definition That Actually Matters for Engineering

A hallucination is when the model generates content that is **confident-sounding but factually
incorrect, fabricated, or inconsistent with the source material.**

The word "hallucination" comes from the AI safety literature. In production AI engineering, it's
your most persistent adversary. Not a rare edge case — a structural property of how LLMs work.

### Why "Bug" Is the Wrong Mental Model

Calling hallucinations a "bug" implies they can be fully fixed with better code. They can't.
They're a consequence of the fundamental training objective:

**An LLM is trained to generate the most plausible next token, not the most accurate one.**

Plausible and accurate correlate strongly most of the time — but they're not the same thing.
When the model doesn't know something, it generates text that looks like a correct answer,
because that's what a correct answer would look like in the training data. There's no "I don't
know" built into the prediction objective.

### The Two Fundamental Types

**Factuality hallucination:** The model states something false as if it were true.
```
User: "Who won the 1987 Nobel Prize in Chemistry?"
Model: "The 1987 Nobel Prize in Chemistry was awarded to Donald Cram and Jean-Marie Lehn
        for their development of crown ethers and cryptands."
[Correct — this is real]

User: "Who won the 1987 Nobel Prize in Literature?"
Model: "The 1987 Nobel Prize in Literature was awarded to Gabriel García Márquez."
[Wrong — it was Joseph Brodsky. García Márquez won in 1982.]
```

**Faithfulness hallucination:** The model generates content inconsistent with the provided context.
```
User: [pastes a document about Company X] "Summarize the financial results."
Model: "Company X reported $2.3B in revenue, up 15% year-over-year."
[Document says $1.8B, up 8%. The model invented different numbers.]
```

These have different root causes and different fixes.

## 2. How It Actually Works — Deep Mechanics

### 2.1 Why Factuality Hallucinations Are Structural

The training objective is:

```
maximize P(next_token | context)
```

The model learns that "The Nobel Prize in Literature was awarded to [Author Name]" is a
high-probability pattern. It has seen this pattern hundreds of times with different names.
When asked about a specific year, it retrieves the pattern + fills in the most probable author
name given the context — which is not necessarily the correct one for that year.

**The rarity problem:** Rare facts appear few times in training data. The model has weak signal
on them. It fills in with the most plausible-sounding answer instead of admitting uncertainty.

**The confidence problem:** The model has no calibrated uncertainty mechanism in the generation loop.
It produces tokens with a probability distribution but doesn't have a way to say "I'm only 30%
confident" unless it's been specifically trained to express uncertainty.

**The knowledge boundary problem:** The model doesn't know what it doesn't know. It has no
internal "confidence about my knowledge" signal — just "what token is most likely next?"

### 2.2 Why Faithfulness Hallucinations Happen

Even with source material in context, models can generate content that contradicts it. Reasons:

1. **Context window attention dilution:** With a very long context, the model's attention to specific
   facts weakens. It partially "forgets" specific numbers while retaining the general pattern.

2. **Prior probability override:** If the model's training strongly established a prior (e.g., most
   company revenue reports use round numbers), that prior can override specific numbers in the context.

3. **Extractive vs abstractive tension:** Summarization models are trained on abstract summaries
   that paraphrase. They sometimes paraphrase too aggressively and change meaning.

4. **Position bias in long contexts:** Facts buried in the middle of long documents are attended
   to less reliably (the "lost in the middle" problem).

### 2.3 A Comprehensive Taxonomy of Hallucination Types

Based on recent 2025 research:

| Type | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Entity fabrication** | Invented names, titles, dates | "The paper was written by Dr. James Wilson at MIT" (person doesn't exist) |
| **Relationship hallucination** | Wrong connections between real entities | "Elon Musk founded Google" |
| **Temporal hallucination** | Wrong dates, sequences | "Einstein won the Nobel in 1918" (he won in 1921) |
| **Numerical hallucination** | Wrong numbers, statistics | "$2.3B revenue" when source says "$1.8B" |
| **Citation fabrication** | Invented papers, books, URLs | "According to Smith et al. (2019)..." (paper doesn't exist) |
| **Instruction-ignoring hallucination** | Generates despite context contradicting it | Summarizes a document about cats with information about dogs |
| **Self-contradiction** | Contradicts itself within a single response | Says "X is true" then later "X is false" in same output |
| **Post-cutoff hallucination** | States guessed information beyond knowledge cutoff | Claims a 2025 event happened when trained through 2024 |

### 2.4 Mitigation Strategies — From Easiest to Most Robust

**Strategy 1: Prompt-level mitigation (fast, partial)**

```
"Only use information from the provided documents. If you don't know the answer
from the documents, say 'I don't have this information in the provided sources.'"

"If you're uncertain about a fact, say so explicitly rather than guessing."

"After giving your answer, cite which part of the document supports each claim."
```

Effectiveness: moderate. Reduces hallucinations but doesn't eliminate them. The model can still
confabulate even when instructed not to.

**Strategy 2: RAG — Ground the model in retrieved facts (strong)**

Instead of relying on the model's training-time memory, retrieve relevant information and put it
in context:

```
User question → retrieve relevant documents → put documents in context →
instruct model to answer ONLY from documents → verify citations
```

This shifts the problem: the model no longer needs to recall facts from training; it reads them
from context. Faithfulness hallucinations remain possible; factuality hallucinations drop significantly.

**Strategy 3: Tool use — Delegate lookup to reliable sources (strongest for facts)**

For factual queries, use tools:
```
User: "What is Apple's current market cap?"
System: calls search tool → gets current data → model synthesizes verified info
```

The model doesn't need to recall; it looks up. This is how ChatGPT with web browsing and Claude
with web search handle time-sensitive factual queries.

**Strategy 4: Constitutional prompting / self-critique**

Ask the model to generate, then critique its own output:
```
Step 1: Generate an answer
Step 2: "Review your answer. Are there any claims you're not confident about?"
Step 3: "Revise your answer to remove or qualify uncertain claims."
```

Moderately effective. The model has some ability to detect its own confabulation under scrutiny.

**Strategy 5: Chain-of-thought + source citation**

Force the model to cite its reasoning:
```
"Answer the question step by step. For each factual claim, indicate:
(a) whether it comes from the provided documents, and (b) your confidence level."
```

**Strategy 6: Ensemble + verification (most robust, most expensive)**

Generate multiple responses, check for consistency. Inconsistencies signal potential hallucinations.
Use a separate verifier model or fact-checking tool.

## 3. Real-World Context

### The Production Hallucination Taxonomy by Severity

| Severity | Type | Example | Impact |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Critical** | Medical/legal/financial fabrication | Wrong drug dosage, invented legal precedent | Patient harm, legal liability |
| **High** | Citation fabrication | Invented paper with plausible author/title | Academic fraud, trust destruction |
| **Medium** | Numerical error | Wrong statistics in a business report | Bad decisions based on fabricated data |
| **Low** | Style/tone inconsistency | Slight factual drift in a creative summary | Minor quality issue |

### What AI Engineering Teams Actually Do

In production LLM applications, mitigation usually combines:

1. **RAG** for knowledge-intensive queries (most effective)
2. **Structured output** with source citations (forces the model to reference inputs)
3. **Confidence thresholds** — if the model expresses uncertainty, route to human review
4. **LLM-as-judge** (covered in Phase 9) — a second model checks the output for hallucinations
5. **Domain restriction** — limit the model's scope so it can only answer from provided context

No single strategy eliminates hallucinations. Defense-in-depth is the approach.

### The Sycophancy-Hallucination Interaction

When you push back on a hallucinated answer ("Are you sure? I thought it was different..."),
aligned models often capitulate — abandoning a correct answer. When you push back on a hallucinated
answer, they might become more confident in the wrong answer or switch to a different wrong answer.

**Practical implication:** Never use user pushback as a verification mechanism. Use tool use,
RAG, or explicit citation requirements instead.

## 4. Code Examples

### Hallucination-Resistant Prompt Pattern

```python
import anthropic

client = anthropic.Anthropic()

def answer_with_grounding(question: str, documents: list[str]) -> dict:
    """
    Forces the model to answer only from provided documents
    and cite specific sources.
    """
    doc_text = "\n\n".join([
        f"[Document {i+1}]:\n{doc}" for i, doc in enumerate(documents)
    ])
    
    system_prompt = """You are a precise research assistant. Your rules:
1. Answer ONLY using information from the provided documents.
2. If the answer is not in the documents, say: "This information is not in the provided documents."
3. After each factual claim, cite the document number: [Doc X].
4. Do not add information from your training that is not in the documents.
5. If documents conflict, note the conflict."""
    
    user_message = f"""Documents:
{doc_text}

Question: {question}"""
    
    response = client.messages.create(
        model="claude-sonnet-4-6",
        max_tokens=1024,
        system=system_prompt,
        messages=[{"role": "user", "content": user_message}]
    )
    
    answer = response.content[0].text
    
    # Simple heuristic: check if answer claims to use documents
    has_citations = "[Doc " in answer
    claims_no_info = "not in the provided documents" in answer.lower()
    
    return {
        "answer": answer,
        "has_citations": has_citations,
        "claimed_no_info": claims_no_info,
        "input_tokens": response.usage.input_tokens
    }


# Example usage
docs = [
    "Q3 2024 Revenue: $1.82 billion, up 8% year-over-year. Net profit margin: 18.3%.",
    "The company launched three new products in Q3: ModelX, DataStream, and CloudSync."
]

result = answer_with_grounding(
    question="What was the revenue growth rate and which products were launched?",
    documents=docs
)
print(result["answer"])
# Should cite [Doc 1] for revenue, [Doc 2] for products
# Should NOT invent numbers or additional products
```

### Self-Consistency Check for Factual Claims

```python
import anthropic
import re
from collections import Counter

client = anthropic.Anthropic()

def consistency_check(question: str, n_samples: int = 5) -> dict:
    """
    Sample N responses, check for consistency.
    High variance = likely hallucination territory.
    Low variance = more reliable answer.
    """
    responses = []
    
    for _ in range(n_samples):
        resp = client.messages.create(
            model="claude-haiku-4-5-20251001",   # cheaper for sampling
            max_tokens=100,
            messages=[{"role": "user", "content": question}],
            temperature=0.7   # some randomness to detect variance
        )
        responses.append(resp.content[0].text.strip())
    
    # Simple heuristic: count how often each response appears
    response_counts = Counter(responses)
    most_common, most_common_count = response_counts.most_common(1)[0]
    
    agreement_rate = most_common_count / n_samples
    
    return {
        "question": question,
        "responses": responses,
        "most_common_answer": most_common,
        "agreement_rate": agreement_rate,
        "is_consistent": agreement_rate >= 0.6,  # 60%+ agreement threshold
        "reliability_signal": "HIGH" if agreement_rate >= 0.8 else
                              "MEDIUM" if agreement_rate >= 0.6 else "LOW"
    }

# Test on a fact vs a hallucination-prone question
fact_q = "What year was Python programming language first released?"
uncertain_q = "What was the exact revenue of OpenAI in Q2 2021?"

fact_result = consistency_check(fact_q)
print(f"'{fact_q}'")
print(f"Agreement: {fact_result['agreement_rate']:.0%}, Reliability: {fact_result['reliability_signal']}")

uncertain_result = consistency_check(uncertain_q)
print(f"\n'{uncertain_q}'")
print(f"Agreement: {uncertain_result['agreement_rate']:.0%}, Reliability: {uncertain_result['reliability_signal']}")
# Well-known facts → high consistency
# Unknown/private data → low consistency (model guesses differently each time)
```

### Citation Verification Pattern

```python
import anthropic
import re

client = anthropic.Anthropic()

def generate_with_citation_verification(question: str, source_text: str) -> dict:
    """
    Two-step process:
    1. Generate answer with citations
    2. Verify each cited claim is actually in the source
    """
    
    # Step 1: Generate answer with inline citations
    answer_response = client.messages.create(
        model="claude-sonnet-4-6",
        max_tokens=512,
        system="Answer questions using only the provided text. Mark each claim with a direct quote from the source in [QUOTE: ...] brackets.",
        messages=[{
            "role": "user",
            "content": f"Source text:\n{source_text}\n\nQuestion: {question}"
        }]
    )
    answer = answer_response.content[0].text
    
    # Step 2: Extract quotes and verify they exist in source
    quoted_claims = re.findall(r'\[QUOTE: (.*?)\]', answer, re.DOTALL)
    
    verification_results = []
    for quote in quoted_claims:
        # Check if the quote (or a close version) exists in the source
        normalized_quote = " ".join(quote.lower().split())
        normalized_source = " ".join(source_text.lower().split())
        is_grounded = normalized_quote in normalized_source
        verification_results.append({
            "quote": quote[:100],
            "verified": is_grounded
        })
    
    all_verified = all(r["verified"] for r in verification_results)
    
    return {
        "answer": answer,
        "citations": verification_results,
        "all_citations_verified": all_verified,
        "hallucination_risk": "LOW" if all_verified else "HIGH"
    }
```

## 6. Alternatives & Comparisons

### Mitigation Strategy Effectiveness

| Strategy | Factuality | Faithfulness | Cost | Complexity |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Better prompting | Moderate | Moderate | None | Low |
| Chain-of-thought | Low-Moderate | Low | Low | Low |
| RAG | High | High | Medium | Medium |
| Tool use / web search | Very High (for facts) | N/A | Medium-High | High |
| LLM-as-judge | Detection only | Detection only | High | Medium |
| Ensemble + voting | High | High | Very High | High |

**No single strategy is sufficient for high-stakes applications.** The industry standard is layering:
RAG + citation requirements + LLM-as-judge verification for anything where factual accuracy is critical.

### Newer Models vs Older Models on Hallucinations

Each model generation is less hallucinatory than the previous, but hallucinations persist:

- GPT-4 (2023): ~25% hallucination rate on challenging factual benchmarks
- GPT-4o (2024): Better, but still hallucinates on rare facts
- Claude 3.5 Sonnet (2024): Strong factuality; still hallucinates on obscure facts
- Current frontier models (2026): Significantly better; long-tail factual errors remain

"Better models hallucinate less" is true but misleading. They also get deployed on more complex tasks
where hallucinations matter more. The engineering need for mitigation doesn't go away.

## 7. Trade-offs

### More Context vs Better Accuracy

More context gives the model more to work with — but also more to confuse it. A 200K token context
with mostly irrelevant documents can make faithfulness hallucinations *worse* because the model
attends to many things, not all relevant.

**Rule:** RAG should retrieve the most relevant chunks, not the most chunks.

### Stricter Prompting vs Response Quality

Heavily constraining the model ("ONLY use documents, NEVER add anything else") reduces hallucinations
but also makes responses more stilted and less helpful for synthesis tasks. Find the right balance
for your use case — 100% grounding for high-stakes facts, looser grounding for creative synthesis.

## 8. When To Use vs. When To Avoid

### High-Stakes Hallucination Mitigation (Medical, Legal, Financial)
✅ Always: RAG + citation requirements + human review for critical decisions
✅ Always: LLM-as-judge or factual checker as a second pass
✅ Always: Clear communication to users about AI limitations
❌ Never: Deploy without telling users the model can be wrong
❌ Never: Use raw LLM output for legal, medical, or financial advice without human review

### General Applications
✅ Use RAG when: Factual accuracy is important and you have reliable source documents
✅ Use tool use when: Time-sensitive or constantly-changing facts (prices, news, weather)
✅ Use chain-of-thought when: Reasoning tasks where showing work helps (not just factual recall)
❌ Don't over-engineer when: Creative tasks where hallucination is acceptable (brainstorming, fiction)

⚠️ Common mistakes:
- Assuming alignment makes models factually accurate — it doesn't
- Building RAG but not testing retrieval quality — garbage retrieved = garbage generated
- Trusting LLM self-reports of confidence — models are poorly calibrated
- Using sycophantic responses as a sign of correctness — they're not
- Not putting source verification in your eval suite — you won't know your hallucination rate

## 9. Senior AI Engineer Perspective

### The Most Important Mindset Shift

Stop thinking about hallucination as "the model making stuff up" and start thinking about it as
"the model completing patterns from its training distribution in the absence of reliable signal."

This reframe tells you the fix: give the model reliable signal. That's RAG. That's tool use.
That's retrieval-augmented, grounded context. The model is doing its best with what it has;
your job as an engineer is to give it better inputs.

### Evals Are the Only Real Defense

You cannot eliminate hallucinations. You can measure them and contain their impact.

Build an eval suite that tests your specific application's hallucination risks:
- For a customer support bot: test on known correct/incorrect claim pairs from your docs
- For a research assistant: test on questions whose answers require exact numbers or citations
- For a code assistant: test on functions with known correct implementations

Run this eval suite on every model update, every prompt change, every RAG pipeline change.
Without evals, you're flying blind — and hallucinations will slip through to users.

### The Citation Requirement Is Underused

The single cheapest and most effective prompt-level mitigation:

```
"For every factual claim, cite the specific document and section you're drawing from.
If you cannot cite a source from the provided documents, say 'I cannot verify this.'"
```

This forces the model into a grounded mode. It also gives downstream systems something to verify.
It's not perfect, but it catches a large fraction of hallucinations with zero engineering cost.
