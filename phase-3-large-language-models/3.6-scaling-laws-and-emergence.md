## 1. What Is It? (Theory)

### Scaling Laws: The Predictable Part

A **scaling law** describes how model performance changes as you increase model size (parameters),
training data, or compute. The discovery that these relationships are **smooth and predictable**
was one of the most important findings in modern AI.

Before scaling laws, model development felt like alchemy: you'd train a bigger model and hope it
was better. Sometimes it was, sometimes it wasn't. There was no reliable way to predict whether
a 10× more expensive training run would produce a 10× better model.

Kaplan et al. (2020) at OpenAI showed that **loss follows a power law** with each scaling dimension:

```
L(N) ∝ N^(-α)    (loss as function of model parameters N)
L(D) ∝ D^(-β)    (loss as function of training data D)
L(C) ∝ C^(-γ)    (loss as function of compute C)

Where α ≈ 0.076, β ≈ 0.095, γ ≈ 0.050 (empirically measured)
```

Translated: **every time you double compute, loss decreases by a predictable, constant factor.**

This wasn't obvious before measurement. The smooth relationship means:
- You can estimate how good a model will be before training it
- You can set a compute budget and predict the resulting model quality
- You can make rational business decisions about training runs

### Emergence: The Unpredictable Part

**Emergent capabilities** are qualitative abilities that appear suddenly when a model crosses
certain scale thresholds — capabilities that were essentially absent at smaller scale and then
appear sharply at larger scale.

```
Performance on "3-digit addition" task:

5M params:    ~5%    (random guessing)
50M params:   ~7%    (barely better)
500M params:  ~20%   (improving but unreliable)
5B params:    ~80%   (sharp jump)
50B params:   ~97%   (near-perfect)

This "sharp jump" is emergence. It didn't gradually improve —
it went from "can't do it" to "can do it" abruptly.
```

Wei et al. (2022) documented dozens of such emergent behaviors — tasks where models showed near-zero
performance below a threshold and high performance above it.

### Why This Matters for AI Engineering

**Scaling laws** mean you can reason about model capabilities before spending on compute. You can
estimate whether a planned training run is worth it.

**Emergence** means that capabilities you didn't train for and didn't expect can appear in larger
models. It's why GPT-3 could do few-shot learning even though nobody specifically trained it to
do few-shot learning. It's why large models are often capable of tasks their creators didn't anticipate.

It also means: **benchmarks that score a small model at 0% might not be meaningful** — they don't tell
you whether the capability is genuinely absent or just below the emergence threshold.

## 2. How It Actually Works — Deep Mechanics

### 2.1 The Kaplan Scaling Laws (2020)

```
Key findings:
1. Loss scales as a power law with compute:
   L ∝ C^(-0.050)
   
2. The optimal allocation of compute C is roughly:
   N (parameters) ∝ C^(0.73)
   D (tokens)      ∝ C^(0.27)
   
   → Double compute → increase model size by ~1.66× AND data by ~1.20×
   → But the ratio favors bigger models

3. The learning curves for loss are smooth and predictable
   → No phase transitions, no surprises in training dynamics
```

**What teams did with this:** When training GPT-3 (2020), OpenAI used scaling laws to predict
that 175B parameters trained on 300B tokens would achieve specific benchmark performance.
The prediction was correct. This was the first time a major AI training run was predicted
before execution.

### 2.2 Chinchilla Scaling Laws (2022)

Hoffmann et al. (DeepMind, 2022) found that Kaplan et al. were wrong about the optimal ratio.
Earlier work trained models that were **too large** for their data budget.

```
The Chinchilla finding:
  For compute-optimal training:
  N (parameters) ≈ D (training tokens)
  
  In other words: train a model with roughly equal parameter count and token count.
  
  Example:
    GPT-3: 175B parameters, 300B tokens → 1.7 tokens per parameter (undertrained)
    Chinchilla: 70B parameters, 1.4T tokens → 20 tokens per parameter (optimal)
    
    Chinchilla (70B, 1.4T tokens) outperforms GPT-3 (175B, 300B tokens)
    despite being 2.5× smaller.
```

**Why earlier models were undertrained:** Compute budgets were spent on bigger models, but bigger
models need proportionally more data to reach their potential. GPT-3 was "underfed."

**The Chinchilla influence on LLaMA:**
Meta's LLaMA models were specifically designed around Chinchilla insights:
- LLaMA 1 (2023): 7B–65B models trained on 1T tokens (higher than Chinchilla optimal, intentional)
- LLaMA 2 (2023): 7B–70B trained on 2T tokens
- LLaMA 3 (2024): 8B–405B trained on 15T+ tokens (far beyond Chinchilla optimal)

**Why go beyond Chinchilla optimal?**

Sardana et al. (2023) showed Chinchilla is **training-compute optimal**, not **inference-compute optimal**.
If you're going to serve a model to millions of users, a smaller model trained on more data is
better: inference runs millions of times, training runs once. A 7B model trained on 15T tokens
is cheaper to serve than a 70B model trained on 1.4T tokens — and can be competitive in quality.

### 2.3 What Are Emergent Capabilities, Really?

Wei et al. (2022) identified many emergent capabilities. Examples:

| Capability | Emergence Scale | What It Does |
|---|---|---|
| Few-shot learning | ~13B params | Learn from examples in the prompt without fine-tuning |
| Chain-of-thought reasoning | ~60–100B params | Step-by-step reasoning when prompted |
| Multi-step arithmetic | ~13B params | 3-digit, then 4-digit arithmetic |
| Word disambiguation | ~13B params | Understand context-dependent word meaning |
| Code debugging | ~60B params | Identify and fix code bugs |
| Analogical reasoning | ~100B params | A:B :: C:? problems |

**The debate on emergence (2023–2026):**

Schaeffer et al. (2023) challenged the emergence finding: they argued that emergence is an artifact
of **measurement**. If you use a discontinuous metric (like exact match accuracy), small improvements
in the underlying probability distribution look like sudden jumps. Using smoother metrics often
reveals gradual improvement all along.

**The current consensus:** Both sides are partly right.
- Some "emergent" capabilities are indeed artifacts of coarse metrics
- But some capabilities do show qualitatively sharp transitions — chain-of-thought reasoning
  is genuinely weak below ~60B parameters and strong above, not just a measurement artifact
- The engineering implication is the same either way: expect qualitative capability differences
  across model scales, even if the underlying trend is smooth

### 2.4 Scaling Laws in 2025–2026: Beyond Chinchilla

The post-Chinchilla era has added new dimensions to scaling:

**Inference-optimal scaling:**
Given a deployment context (X million requests/day), what's the cheapest model to train that
meets quality requirements? Answer: smaller model, more data — the opposite of Kaplan's prescription.

**Data quality scaling:**
FineWeb (2024) showed that 15T tokens of quality-filtered web text trains a model that outperforms
one trained on 30T tokens of unfiltered web text. Data quality is a scaling dimension.

**Compute scaling for reasoning (o1, R1):**
OpenAI's o1 showed a new scaling axis: **inference compute**. More thinking time (longer
chain-of-thought at inference) improves accuracy on hard problems. You can trade inference
compute for quality, not just training compute.

```
New scaling axes:
  Training: parameters × data × compute  (Kaplan/Chinchilla)
  Data quality: filtered > raw at same size
  Inference: more tokens of thinking → better answers on hard tasks
  Multimodal: image + text + audio > text-only
```

### 2.5 The Return on Compute

A practical framing for AI engineers:

```
Budget decision: $10M training run

Kaplan (2020):     train as large a model as possible on available data
Chinchilla (2022): balance model size and data equally  
Inference-optimal: train smallest model that meets quality on most data
(2023+)

Rule of thumb for 2026:
  If you'll serve to > 1M users: train a smaller model on much more data
  If you're doing research: optimize for quality at fixed compute (Chinchilla)
  If you need bleeding-edge capability: scale up everything
```

## 3. Real-World Context

### How Scaling Laws Shaped the Industry

| Year | Development | Scaling Insight |
|---|---|---|
| 2020 | GPT-3 (OpenAI) | "Bigger models work, and it's predictable" |
| 2022 | Chinchilla (DeepMind) | "Earlier models were undertrained; balance matters" |
| 2022 | LLaMA 1 (Meta) | "Open release; small models trained longer are competitive" |
| 2023 | Mixtral (Mistral) | "MoE: more total params, same active params = better tradeoff" |
| 2023 | o1 (OpenAI) | "Inference scaling: think longer = better on hard problems" |
| 2024 | DeepSeek-V3 | "671B MoE model at a fraction of GPT-4 training cost; data+efficiency" |
| 2025 | LLaMA 4 (Meta) | "MoE + massive data + long context; open frontier model" |

### The Bitter Lesson (Richard Sutton, 2019)

Before scaling laws were formally measured, Richard Sutton wrote "The Bitter Lesson":

> "The biggest lesson that can be read from 70 years of AI research is that general methods
> that leverage computation are ultimately the most effective... Researchers always tried to
> make systems that worked the way we thought our minds worked — chess programs that tried
> to search the way humans seemed to search, vision programs that tried to recognize the
> way humans seemed to recognize. But scale + simple methods > clever human-engineered approaches."

This captured the spirit of what scaling laws later confirmed mathematically. The "bitter" part:
human-designed domain knowledge is repeatedly beaten by just adding more compute and data.

For AI engineers, this means: when in doubt, scale before cleverness.

### What This Means for Your Product Decisions

**Choosing between models:**

"Use the biggest model you can afford" is wrong in 2026. The right question is:
"What's the smallest model that reliably passes my eval suite for this task?"

A LLaMA 3 8B model fine-tuned on your task can outperform GPT-4 on that specific task at
1/50th the inference cost. Scaling laws explain why: the 8B model was trained on 15T tokens
and fine-tuned on exactly your domain — its knowledge for your task may be richer than the
general-purpose larger model.

**On emergence and benchmarks:**

When a benchmark reports that "GPT-4 achieves 90% on X" and "GPT-3.5 achieves 60%", the
gap isn't linear. GPT-3.5 may be fine for 80% of your use cases. Don't pay 5× inference cost
for 30% more accuracy unless your use case requires it. Run your own evals.

## 4. Code Examples

### Plotting a Simple Scaling Law

```python
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

def compute_optimal_loss(params_millions: float, alpha: float = 0.076) -> float:
    """
    Simple power-law approximation.
    Loss decreases predictably with parameters (other things equal).
    """
    return 3.5 * (params_millions / 1.0) ** (-alpha)  # arbitrary normalization

# Plot the scaling relationship
param_sizes = np.logspace(2, 6, 100)  # 100M to 1T parameters
losses = [compute_optimal_loss(p / 1e6) for p in param_sizes]

fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(12, 4))

# Linear scale
axes[0].plot([p/1e9 for p in param_sizes], losses)
axes[0].set_xlabel("Parameters (billions)")
axes[0].set_ylabel("Loss")
axes[0].set_title("Loss vs Scale (linear)")

# Log-log scale (scaling law appears as straight line)
axes[1].loglog(param_sizes, losses)
axes[1].set_xlabel("Parameters")
axes[1].set_ylabel("Loss")
axes[1].set_title("Loss vs Scale (log-log) — straight line = power law")

plt.tight_layout()
plt.savefig("scaling_law.png", dpi=150)
print("Saved scaling_law.png")

# Mark known models approximately
model_params = {
    "GPT-2": 1.5e8, "GPT-3": 1.75e11, "GPT-4 (est)": 1e12,
    "LLaMA 3 8B": 8e9, "LLaMA 3 70B": 7e10,
}
for name, params in model_params.items():
    loss = compute_optimal_loss(params / 1e6)
    print(f"{name:20s}: {params/1e9:.1f}B params → estimated loss {loss:.3f}")
```

### Chinchilla Optimal Compute Allocation

```python
def chinchilla_optimal_allocation(compute_budget_flops: float) -> dict:
    """
    Given a compute budget in FLOPs, compute the Chinchilla-optimal
    model size and training token count.
    
    Chinchilla rule: N_optimal ≈ sqrt(C / 6)
    where C = total training compute in FLOPs.
    """
    # Chinchilla: roughly equal budget to model size and data
    # A forward+backward pass ≈ 6N FLOPs (for N parameters)
    # So: total_flops ≈ 6 * N * D
    # Chinchilla optimum: N ≈ D
    # Therefore: 6 * N^2 ≈ C → N ≈ sqrt(C/6)
    
    optimal_params = (compute_budget_flops / 6) ** 0.5
    optimal_tokens = optimal_params  # Chinchilla: tokens ≈ params
    
    return {
        "compute_budget_petaflops": compute_budget_flops / 1e15,
        "optimal_params_billions":  optimal_params / 1e9,
        "optimal_tokens_billions":  optimal_tokens / 1e9,
        "tokens_per_param": optimal_tokens / optimal_params,  # ≈ 1 for Chinchilla
    }

# Example: GPT-3 training budget
# GPT-3: 175B params × 300B tokens × 6 FLOPs per param per token ≈ 3.1×10²³ FLOPs
gpt3_compute = 175e9 * 300e9 * 6  # FLOPs

chinchilla_result = chinchilla_optimal_allocation(gpt3_compute)
print(f"With GPT-3's compute budget:")
print(f"  Chinchilla-optimal params: {chinchilla_result['optimal_params_billions']:.0f}B")
print(f"  Chinchilla-optimal tokens: {chinchilla_result['optimal_tokens_billions']:.0f}B")
print(f"  (vs actual GPT-3: 175B params, 300B tokens)")
# Chinchilla says: ~70B params on ~1.4T tokens would have been better than 175B on 300B
```

### Detecting Capability Thresholds in Your Evals

```python
import anthropic
import json
from typing import Callable

client = anthropic.Anthropic()

def benchmark_capability(
    task_generator: Callable[[int], dict],
    n_samples: int = 20,
    model: str = "claude-haiku-4-5-20251001"
) -> dict:
    """
    Tests a capability across N samples.
    Returns accuracy and confidence interval.
    """
    correct = 0
    results = []
    
    for i in range(n_samples):
        task = task_generator(i)
        
        response = client.messages.create(
            model=model,
            max_tokens=100,
            messages=[{"role": "user", "content": task["prompt"]}]
        )
        
        answer = response.content[0].text.strip()
        is_correct = task["check"](answer)
        correct += is_correct
        results.append({"task": task["prompt"][:50], "correct": is_correct})
    
    accuracy = correct / n_samples
    # 95% confidence interval (Wilson score)
    z = 1.96
    n = n_samples
    ci = z * (accuracy * (1 - accuracy) / n) ** 0.5
    
    return {
        "accuracy": accuracy,
        "ci_95": ci,
        "n": n_samples,
        "results": results
    }

# Example: arithmetic task generators
def three_digit_addition(seed: int) -> dict:
    import random
    rng = random.Random(seed)
    a = rng.randint(100, 999)
    b = rng.randint(100, 999)
    return {
        "prompt": f"What is {a} + {b}? Answer with just the number.",
        "check": lambda ans: str(a + b) in ans
    }

def five_digit_addition(seed: int) -> dict:
    import random
    rng = random.Random(seed)
    a = rng.randint(10000, 99999)
    b = rng.randint(10000, 99999)
    return {
        "prompt": f"What is {a} + {b}? Answer with just the number.",
        "check": lambda ans: str(a + b) in ans
    }

# Run benchmarks
print("3-digit addition:")
result = benchmark_capability(three_digit_addition, n_samples=10)
print(f"  Accuracy: {result['accuracy']:.0%} ± {result['ci_95']:.0%}")

print("5-digit addition:")
result = benchmark_capability(five_digit_addition, n_samples=10)
print(f"  Accuracy: {result['accuracy']:.0%} ± {result['ci_95']:.0%}")
# 5-digit arithmetic should be harder — demonstrates why task difficulty matters
# And why benchmarking your specific use case beats published benchmarks
```

## 6. Alternatives & Comparisons

### Scaling Dimensions (2026)

| Dimension | Effect | Limit |
|---|---|---|
| **Model parameters** | More capacity, better knowledge | Diminishing returns; inference cost grows |
| **Training tokens** | Better knowledge density; better common-sense | Data quality bottleneck |
| **Context window** | Longer documents; better coherence | KV-cache memory; attention quality |
| **Inference tokens** (chain-of-thought) | Better reasoning on hard problems | Cost; latency |
| **Data quality** | Better capability at same size | Quality filtering is hard to scale |
| **Multimodality** | Cross-modal understanding | Architecture complexity |

### The Capability vs Alignment Tradeoff at Scale

Bigger models are more capable AND harder to align:
- More capable: harder to write comprehensive refusal policies for a wider capability set
- Better at following instructions: but also better at following harmful instructions
- Better at reasoning: but also better at reasoning around safety guidelines

This is one reason why Anthropic, OpenAI, and DeepMind invest heavily in alignment research
alongside capability research. The two must advance together.

## 7. Trade-offs

### Training Bigger vs Training More Data
Training on more data at the same model size:
**Pros:** Cheaper inference (smaller model), better knowledge density, inference-optimal
**Cons:** More data processing cost, diminishing returns eventually

Training bigger at same data:
**Pros:** More capacity, potentially emergent capabilities at scale
**Cons:** Expensive inference, potentially overtrained (undertrained in Chinchilla sense)

### Using Public Benchmarks vs Your Own Evals
**Published benchmarks** (MMLU, HumanEval, etc.):
- Easy to compare across models
- May not reflect your specific use case
- Models are increasingly trained/evaluated against public benchmarks (contamination risk)

**Custom evals on your task:**
- Directly measures what you care about
- Effort to create; requires domain expertise
- Cannot be gamed by benchmark-aware training

Rule: use published benchmarks for rough model selection; use custom evals to decide which model
actually ships for your use case.

## 8. When To Use vs. When To Avoid

### Choosing Model Size Based on Scaling Laws
✅ Use a larger model when: The task requires broad knowledge, complex reasoning, or rare capability
✅ Use a smaller fine-tuned model when: The task is narrow and well-defined, inference cost matters
❌ Don't assume bigger = better for your specific task without running evals
❌ Don't use published benchmark accuracy as a proxy for your use case performance

⚠️ Common mistakes:
- Paying for GPT-4 for tasks a fine-tuned Mistral-7B handles equally well
- Using the smallest available model without testing — capability cliffs are real
- Treating a model's emergent capabilities as guaranteed — evaluate them on your data
- Ignoring inference compute (reasoning tokens) as a quality lever for hard tasks

## 9. Senior AI Engineer Perspective

### The Key Insight About Emergence for Production

Emergence means: **you cannot reliably predict from a small model's performance whether a larger
model will "get it."** A task at 0% on 7B may jump to 80% on 70B. This is non-linear and surprising.

Practical approach: when a small model fails at something your product needs, before concluding
"LLMs can't do this," try a model one order of magnitude larger. The capability might emerge.

But also: emergence doesn't always happen. Some tasks are genuinely hard at any scale because
they require symbolic computation, perfect recall, or exact counting — things transformers aren't
well-suited for architecturally.

### The Inference Scaling Insight Changes Everything

OpenAI's o1 proved that you can trade **inference compute for accuracy**: let the model think longer
with chain-of-thought, and it solves harder problems. This opened a new axis for AI product design:

- For latency-sensitive tasks: use a smaller/faster model
- For accuracy-critical tasks: use a larger model or a reasoning model that thinks longer

In 2026, this means you should ask not just "which model?" but "how much thinking budget?" for each
task in your application. A cheap fast model with 100 output tokens may be fine for simple Q&A;
a reasoning model with 2000 output tokens of thinking may be needed for complex multi-step problems.

### The Benchmark Contamination Problem

Models are increasingly evaluated against the same benchmarks used to train/select them. A model
that scores 90% on MMLU may have been trained on MMLU-like data. Published benchmarks are less
reliable signals than they were three years ago.

For AI engineering decisions: **run your own evals on representative samples of your actual data.**
MMLU doesn't tell you how well GPT-4 will handle your customer support tickets.
